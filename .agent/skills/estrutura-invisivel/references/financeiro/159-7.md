      But that was OK. Mick and Lucy weren't interested in tourists. They wanted to farm. They built a thatched straw cottage and began to cultivate their land. Occasionally, a backpacker would wander by and ask if he could camp for the night. Mick and Lucy were always happy to oblige.       Eventually, the couple built a second cottage to house the passerbys.       "It had a dirt roof," Mick remembers. "We gave the people a kerosene stove and a salt shaker and then left them to fend for themselves."       The travelers continued to come--so Mick and Lucy built a few more cottages...and then a few more. What began with an initial investment of $300 is now, more than 20 years later, one of the most impressive spots in the entire country...an oasis in the jungle called Chaa Creek, with more than 30 cottages, an elegant open-air dining room, a thatched-roof bar, a 
butterfly sanctuary, a museum dedicated to the natural history of Mick and 
Lucy's adopted homeland...even a spa.       At Chaa Creek, you won't find a telephone...or a television or radio or CD player. Here, the eclectic mix of guests relies on the ancient art o f 
conversation for entertainment in the evening...and during the day they're too busy canoeing, horseback riding, hiking, cave-exploring, and climbing nearby Mayan ruins to bemoan the lack of "modern conveniences."
