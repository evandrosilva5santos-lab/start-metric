     Local expatriates are involved in all variety of endeavors. (We mentioned a handful earlier.) One of the easiest ways to start a busine ss in 
Ecuador is to start a small import-export concern. This sort of endeavor gives you an ideal excuse to shop, for starters. Done on a small scale, buying merchandise in markets and shops overseas for resale back home can easily pay for your travel. Done on a larger scale, you can turn this kind of project into a steady income.       Ecuador's artisans are highly skilled. The markets are overflowing with handicrafts, jewelry, sweaters and the like...all on sale for bargain pri ces. 
From turquoise, silver, and leather goods to statuary, fine alpaca apparel , 
and magnificent textiles, you'll find an endless selection of products you might export.       But that is just one business option. The list of potential fields goes on and on, soft ecotourism, extreme sports, textile manufacturing and export, hospitality, real estate development, infrastructure provisioning for upcoming private telecommunications concessions, and much more.  
Two IL entrepreneurs make good in Cuenca   
     I'm sitting at one of Cuenca's most elegant bars, enjoying an excellent country pâté and a glass of Chardonnay. Some British tourists are warming themselves in front of the fire, and there are diners at every table enjoying what I'd describe as the most unique and creative menu in Ecuador.   
     The Eucalyptus Café, which opened to a packed house last week, is the creation of International Living readers Chris and Leslie Breen, who've been in town about seven months. The Eucalyptus is an "international tapas bar" and serves not only traditional tapas, as Page 1 of 8

     Chris and Leslie landed in Cuenca in March with just a bit of Spanish, pending visas, furniture in transit, and a great deal of pioneer spirit. Exhibiting a talent for finding bilingual associates, they pried their belongings out of customs, arranged in-home Spanish lessons, set up a corporation, found a prime historic downtown location, and garnered all of the necessary permits to open their restaurant.   
     Chris, originally from the UK and without prior construction contracting experience, acted as their general contractor and 
personally supervised the restoration of the rundown building into a 
gracious example of Spanish colonial architecture, while Leslie, a Maine native, found and hired the staff, located the ingredients for all their epicurean creations, and helped design the building, working from imagination and magazine photos, which local carpenters, welders, and masons brought to life.   
     Their British draft beer (including an excellent dark porter, called llama negra) is made from ingredients imported from England by brewers that the Breens found in Quito. Their restaurant also boasts a "left luggage" service and an English-language book exchange and map store.   
    Ecuador isn't for everyone,  it's sort of the Dodge City of the 21st century. Chris and Leslie have two of the key ingredients for success here: a pioneering spirit and a can-do attitude. You'll find their Eucalyptus Café at Gran Colombia 9-41.
