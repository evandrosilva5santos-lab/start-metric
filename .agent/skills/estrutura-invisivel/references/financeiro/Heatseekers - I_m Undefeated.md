These are Sabermetric calculations. In reality, there are 230 in total.
Together, they can tell you anything and everything you want to know about any baseball
player from his walk percentage and strikeout percentage, to his speed score, clutch
score, and isolated power.
But out of all these calculations, one of them reigns supreme.
It is the most well-known, influential, and respected metric in baseball today.
It’s called WAR.
In baseball, WAR stands for Wins Above Replacement. It’s a single number that tells you
how many additional wins a baseball player is worth to his team.
Just to give you a rough idea...
The average full-time MLB player has a WAR of about 2. Bench players have a WAR
typically between 0 and 1. All-star players have a WAR of 4-5. And MVPs have a WAR of
6 or higher.
It would take me hours to explain the math that goes into computing a player’s WAR.
You have to measure all of his on-field contributions – his hitting ability, defensive ability,
and base running – and then add adjustments to those contributions based on the
difficulty of his position.
I don’t have time to explain all that. And neither do you.
But here’s an easy way to look at it…
The higher a player’s WAR is...
The higher his contribution is to his team...
The higher his contract price should be.
Mike Trout from the Los Angeles Angels, for example, has a WAR of 8 this season,
making him worth eight additional wins to his team.  
He also costs $34 million a year.
Divide that out and Mike Trout is worth $4.2 million per winning game. This is his Cost Per
WAR or, put another way, what it costs to keep him on the payroll.
Blake Snell of the Tampa Bay Rays, on the other hand, has a WAR rating of +5.3, making
him worth over five additional wins to his team.
In comparison, Snell’s contract is $558,000 a year. His Cost Per WAR is around $105,000,
which is 40 times LESS than what Mike Trout costs.

intricacies of the WAR calculation...
But I can sum it up in a few sentences.
I start by adding up everything a company owns and

profitable, growing its cash flow, reducing its debt,

I then put the company through a credit-strength test

The sum of these variables is then divided by the company’s market cap, debt, minority
interest, preferred stock, and cash positions.
