<h1>What’s it worth?  
I love my job, but I hate bidding. It gives me hives. Or maybe not hives, but it sure does 
make me uncomfortable. How much are words actually worth?  It’s not like I’m selling 
widgets, after all.  
 
When you hire a copywriter there are three ways y ou can set prices: by the project, by the 
hour and (the frightening one) by the word.  
 
I encourage all of my clients to work on a project basis. That way I know how much I’m 
going to be paid, and they know how much to put into budget right from the beginni ng. 
It’s simple, straightforward and, I think, fairest for everybody involved.  
 
Working on a straight hourly basis can be fine for my small jobs, but for anything bigger 
the need to track time and tendency for things to take longer than expected can cause a 
variety of headaches.  
 
As far as by the word pricing; I’ve only heard of anyone doing this twice and, personally, 
think it’s a horrible idea. Copywriting is an art and a skill focused on brevity. Paying by 
the word encourages over -writing and keeps your  company from getting the best material 
it can.  
 
In other words, if a copywriter offers to do a project by the word, put your fingers in your  
ears, run for the door and, if you’ve already exchanged business cards, change your 
phone number. Any writer who  works by the word isn’t someone you want anywhere 
near your business.

<h1>Say it Loud, Say it Proud  
Here’s a quick tip . Get a voice recorder – anything from the old tape deck in your garage 
to an ipod with a microphone attached will do. Now go to your website and record 
yourself reading the copy out loud. Play it back. How does it sound? Smooth and 
conversational or over -technical, stilted and – the worst offense – boring?  
 
With all the pages of text we read everyday, it’s easy to forget that it’s how copy 
“sounds” that matters. Act like everything you write is going to end up on the radio and 
you’ll find yourself crafting  more conversational – and more effective – copy.
