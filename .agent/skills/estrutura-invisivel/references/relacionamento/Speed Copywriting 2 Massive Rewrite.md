4. Basic Things To Know About A Squeeze Page  
 
Right now I’m going to tell you some really basic things you need to know about squeeze

Squeeze Page Basics #1: Ke ep Your Important Information Above the fold  
  
In the newspaper business, when you have a broadsheet piece of newspaper like the New 
York Times or the Miami Herald or the Chicago Tribune or the LA Times, it’s folded in 
half. There’s the masthead at the top  and a bunch of articles, and then on the bottom 
there’s a bunch of articles.  
 
 What they call ‘above the fold,’ if you buy it from a news box, is anything in the 
first half of the newspaper that shows up on the newsstands or when the newspaper’s on 
the ta ble. It’s called “above the fold” because it’s literally above the fold that splits the 
paper in half.  
 
 Now, even though we don’t fold the computer screen (or at least I don’t) that phrase has 
somehow carried over into the internet and we talk about ‘abov e the fold,’ which means 
whatever you can see when you first land on the page without scrolling. Basically all the 
information that’s up near the top of the page is considered “above the fold.”  
 
The first thing you need to know is when you’re trying to ge t someone to give you their 
name and email address is to always keep your opt in box above the fold. This is very, 
very important. You can lose a lot of people if they have to do any work or if they have to 
figure out what to do. Once they get to your sale s letter, yes, that’s where we want the 
scrolling down and down and down and digging into your offer, but on your squeeze 
page we want to make it as easy as possible for them to just quickly get excited about the 
promises that you’re making and give you th eir email address.  
 
Squeeze Page Basics #2:  Your Squeeze Page Needs To Be Exciting Visually   
 
Now, this doesn’t mean you need to have all sorts of razzle dazzle graphics and dancing 
frogs and things like that. But your squeeze page shouldn’t look like the department of 
agriculture website. You want a clean, simple and appealing design that drags your 
prospects kicking and screaming to the sign up box.  
 
Squeeze Page Basics #3: Your Squeeze Page Needs Exciting Copy  
 
Now, squeeze pages are sort of the domain of “short copy” in internet marketing. There 
are times you want to use much longer copy (say if you want to make Google happy) in 
your squeeze page, but generally you just want to have a headline and a few bullets. But 
that just means that what little copy  you have has to work really, really hard.  
 
That doesn’t mean having over -the-top, hyped and ridiculous copy. But your copy should 
be scintillating. It should dive right into where the customer’s mind is and make an offer 
that they can’t help but want to know more about.  
 
  
Squeeze Page Basics #4: Your Squeeze Page Needs To Work!  
 
This really should go without saying, but you’d be amazed at how many squeeze pages 
I’ve seen that, when you enter your email address and hit the button, nothing happens. Or 
wors e yet, you’re dragged off to an error page. (And let me tell you, most customers who 
go to an error page never ever come back.)  
 
So please, when you put your squeeze page up, test it. Make sure it works. Make sure the 
email address and name are added to yo ur database, that your bribe is delivered and that 
your customer is scooted along happily to the content you offered or to your sales page.  
 
Squeeze Page Basics #5: Be Non -Threatening  
 
This is very, very important. With the amount of spam going on out ther e today, people 
are very, very scared about their email security and about identity theft. they need to feel 
safe and I’m going to tell you exactly how to make them feel safe later on in today’s 
session.  
 
Squeeze Page Basics #6: Your Squeeze Page Needs To Offer More Than You’re 
Asking For  
 
Again, this is very important. Your squeeze page needs to make an offer where your 
customers feel like they’re basically ripping you off by taking what you’ve got to offer in 
exchange for just their name and their email a ddress. They need to feel like they’re 
getting the good end of the deal.  
 
They need to feel like they HAVE TO give you their email address. They should never be 
asking themselves “Why the heck should I opt in?” Instead, you want your prospects 
looking at y our squeeze page and seeing opting is as an absolute bargain that they just 
can’t wait to do.  
 
So there you have it. You just learned six basic things you need to know in order to create 
squeeze pages that build a valuable list asset for you that makes you  money.  
 
Again, they were:  
 
1. Keep your important information above the fold.  
2. Your squeeze page needs to be visually exciting.  
3. Have scintillating and exciting copy.  
4. Your squeeze page needs to work (duh!)  
5. Your squeeze page should be non -threate ning.

5. The Psychology Of The Squeeze Page  
 
In this section I’m going to tell you how to get into the head of your prospect and find out 
who they really are so that you can write squeeze page copy and make an offer that they 
can’t help but exchange their email address for.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 Before we get into t hat, though, I want to give you one little tip for a problem that 
you’re going to be happy to have. Why? Because when you have this problem, it means 
that your squeeze page is working and that you’re building a nice big list.  
 
If you have enough people on your list it’s easy at a certain point to become calloused 
and removed and distant. All those people who are receiving emails can become just a 
number to you.  
 
 But the people on your list don’t look at it that way from their end. You always 
need to rememb er how they look at it from their end. From their end, they’re giving up 
something really valuable —access to their inbox --and they’re making themselves 
vulnerable, so you want to make sure that it’s really worth their while to opt into your list 
in the fir st place and, ultimately, to stay on your list for the long haul.  
 
I just wanted to get that out of the way. Now let’s really take a look at the psychology of 
the squeeze page and the 3 questions you need to answer in your own mind before 
writing powerful squeeze page copy.  
 
 
Psychology of The Squeeze Part 1: Who is your prospect?  
 
 Here’s the first thing. What do you need to know about the person that you want 
to go from looking at that page to on your list before you even start? What do you need to 
know?  
 
 The first thing you need to know is who they are. I don’t mean their name (though 
if you do your job right, they’ll give you that.)  
 
 What I mean is that you need to know who they are in terms of basic facts about 
them. Maybe it’s demographic information : their age, where they live – probably not that 
– but if you had a regional or local business and you wanted to just collect names of 
people within your service area, which might be a 5 -mile radius or a 50 -mile radius from 
your business location, then tha t information would be important.  
 
Probably though, what you want to know is what are the people in your target audience 
interested in, or what do they want to do (that you’re going to tell them how to do) or 
what “are” they that makes them stand out from the rest of humanity and make them 
someone who you want to sell to?   
 
So that’s the first thing you need to know before writing a squeeze page: Who are you 
trying to talk to?  
 
Psychology of The Squeeze Page Part 2: What is their greatest problem or desire?  
 
Once you know WHO you’re trying to talk to, you need to know what it is that they want. 
What’s their greatest problem (that you’re going to help them solve) or what’s their 
greatest desire around a specific issue. Now, you’re probably not going to solve this 
problem or grant this desire with your free premium on your squeeze page, but you’re 
going to want to sell to these people eventually and you need to know what it is that they 
want.  
 
 
Psychology of The Squeeze Page Part 3: How Do They Talk About their  Problem Or 
Desire?  
 
This is a big one that lot of people really miss. They get the first two parts right but then 
go in with language that’s really far off the mark from how people in this particular niche 
actually talk about their problem.  
 
For example, Frank Kern is a very successful online marketer who’s really famous for an 
ebook he wrote about teaching your parrot to talk.  
 
Who’s his target audience? Parrot owners.  
What’s their greatest desire? To get their parrot to talk.  
How do they talk about it? Very simply. They say “I want my parrot to talk and I can’t 
get it to talk.” Words to that effect.  
 
Once you’ve got answers to these questions really solid in your mind, you can work 
backwards through the sales page, through the autoresponders (which your targeted list is 
going to receive after they sign up), through the bonus or the premium or the special 
report or the bribe, all the way through to the copy and the design elements of the squeeze 
page.  
 
Again, those three big pieces of information you need are: 
 
 Who your audience is  
 What their greatest problem or desire is  
 And how they talk about it.  
 
Seems simple now, doesn’t it? Good, it should.

6. The Bribe  
 
I guess bribe isn’t the only word you could use. You could call it a bonus or a premium. 
But I always like the honesty of a word like “Bribe.” Because that’s really what you’re 
doing, you’re giving your prospect something really valuable to get t hem to take a very 
specific action and to give you something valuable in return.  
 
In this section I’m going to teach you how to create amazing “email bait” bribes that suck 
in email addresses like absolute crazy.  
 
First lets talk about the two big things t hat your bribe needs to accomplish:  
 
What your bribe must accomplish part 1:  
  
 Your bribe has to speak to the inner core emotional problems or desires that your 
prospect has.  
 
Let’s go back to the Frank Kern Parrot example.  
 
If you say “Parrots can talk. Not everyone knows how to do it. Here are five tips,” it’s 
probably not going to speak to their inner desires.  
 
Instead you need to get more into the emotions of the prospect. You need to say 
something like:  
 
 “You have this parrot and it’s driving you cr azy. It’s chattering like anything, like 
a madman, but it won’t say a single word in English. What do you want to do about it?”  
 
 You see, the difference is the person’s frustrated and their desire is high, as 
opposed to in the first one when it’s very, ve ry dry and factual.  
 
So your bribe needs to really go after the emotions of your prospect. That’s part 1.  
 
What your bribe must accomplish part 2:  
 
The second thing that your bribe needs to accomplish is it needs to offer solutions and 
specifics to help yo ur prospect get closer (at least in their mind) to an end to the 
problem or to an attainment of their great desire.  
 
Now, obviously, your bribe isn’t going to completely solve the problem. If you did that in 
your bribe, you wouldn’t have anything left to s ell. But you want to get them closer to 
their ultimate goal.  
 
Also, if you offer a really good bribe, then you’ll basically be priming the pump for your 
product. Why? Because your bribe is sort of the “audition” for your materials. Your 
prospect will look at your bribe and will think that the quality of your bribe is going to 
match the quality of your materials over all. So make sure your bribe is really good.  
 
Two Great Bonus Bribe Tips  
 
Two important things to keep in mind when creating your bribe:  
 
 
1. The title of your bonus or report needs to talk about satisfying your desire or solving 
your problem. Don’t get cute in our title. Say something very simple like “How to get 
your parrot to speak its first word of English in 3 easy steps.”  
 
2.  Second, the content of the report needs to break the problem down into little pieces of 
the problem and little pieces of the desires. Little bite sized chunks. You want your report 
to have kind of a “chunky” feel to it, filling in little pieces of your target’s desire s while 
still being intriguing and compelling along the way.  
 
  
 One important thing to know is the copy in your lead generation and the copy on 
your squeeze page are going to be very hot and provocative and exciting. You need to 
have the same thing all th e way through your sales process and especially in your bribe.  
 
 You can’t say, “Ok, well, I got your name so now I can just relax and be boring 
and hope that you get it.” No, you have to keep that energy level up. You have to be 
consistent with where you were before.  
 
 So those are the main things.  
 
One more thing to cover about your offer and your free report before we move on. It used 
to be that all you had to do was write up a very simple sort of PDF report as a bribe and 
that would do the job for you. But these days there are great opportunities to do a lot 
more than that.  
 
Your offer could be a downloadable audio.  
 
Your offer could be a video that you post online.  
 
Your offer could be an interactive video put together with presentation software like 
articulate presenter.  
 
But no matter what method you choose, your offer has to deliver real value.

Squeeze Page Optimizer #1: Copy  
 
There are three big copy components to your squeeze page.  
 
They are:  
 
 1. Your He adline  
 2. Your Bullets  
 3. Your Opt -in Copy which goes right above the opt -in form.  
 
Let’s take each of these one at a time.  
 
 
Squeeze Page Copy Part 1: Your Headline  
 
Your headline on your squeeze page has one big job, and that’s to transition you from 
whatever you clicked on to get to the squeeze page into your offer and your mindset.  
 
After all, your customers aren’t just stumbling onto your squeeze page. They’re getting 
there by clicking on an adwords ad, or clicking a link in an email, or clicking a l ink at the 
bottom of an article or typing in a URL after seeing an ad out in the bright and scary real 
world.  
 
So your headline has to segue from the ad that was placed. It has to provide an answer  to 
whatever promise you made in your lead generation ad.  
 
When they clicked an ad that said, say “Teach Your Parrot How To Talk” it showed that 
they wanted more information. So the job of the squeeze page and the job of your 
headline is to basically pique that curiosity. It needs to grab them and basically force  
them to read the rest of your squeeze page.  
 
Now, this is very similar to how you write a headline for a regular sales page, but the big 
difference is that in this case you aren’t selling a product, you’re selling what they’re 
going to learn in the bribe or what they’re going to learn when they click the “Free 
Instant Access” button and go gallivanting into your site.  
 
Got it?  
 
Great, let’s move no to bullets:  
 
Squeeze Page Copy Part 2: Your Bullets  
 
In your squeeze page your bullets (or fascinations like some people call them) are 
basically the hot buttons that you’ve discovered about your market. Remember in session 
one when we talked about hot buttons? This is a great opportunity to put them into action. 
Your bullets should tell your reader all the great  stuff they’re going to get when they opt 
in, all the information that you’re going to reveal to them and (either implied or directly) 
the great results that this information is going to give them.  
  
And then, finally, we have the opt in copy.  
 
Squeeze Pag e Copy Part 3: Opt -In Copy  
 
Your opt -in copy is basically your offer. This is where you lay it out and say “If you give 
me your email address, you’ll get access to all of this great information, plus I’ll send you 
a free copy of my special report on how to  make amazing amounts of money teaching 
parrots how to talk.”  
 
So again,  
 
Your headline is your hook that drags people in and makes them read the rest of your 
squeeze page.  
 
Your bullets are your “reasons why.” Your bullets tell the reader what problem y ou’re 
going to solve and get him all excited about the great results you’re offering.  
 
And your opt -in copy is your offer that closes the deal.  
 
Digging Deeper Into Squeeze Page Copy  
 
Let’s forget for just a second that it’s a squeeze page and let’s just c all it a sales page. I 
know we’re not selling anything for money, but let’s say it’s a sales page in this way.  
 
 It’s a sales page because you are convincing somebody to do something and 
you’re asking them to pay money – except it’s not money, it’s their n ame and their email 
address, which as an asset for you is future money – in exchange for something that 
you’re going to give them.  
 
 On a squeeze page, what are you selling? What you’re really selling is the value 
exchange and the beginning of a relations hip. The relationship is between you and the 
prospect. It’s actually between you and a lot of prospects, but it’s a one -on-one 
relationship as far as each prospect is concerned.  
 
 What your headline, your bullets, and your copy need to do is sell the offer , 
which is this free report that you’re giving away, or this free series of reports, or this 
information, or this video, or some templates, or some software, or a free trial – 
something or even just the information they’re going to learn on your amazingly 
information sales page –  
 
 And you’re also selling the possibility of a mutually beneficial relationship.  
 
 Now you’re not going to write a headline that says, “Hey dummy, I’m going to 
give you a free report and you might have a chance to have a relations hip with our 
company!”  
 
 I mean that would be a little too on the nose, as they say in Hollywood.  
 
 But what you are going to say is something that a person reading it could come to 
the conclusion that that would happen.  
 
 You might say, “My parrot couldn’ t talk and I learned these secrets and now it 
can. I’d like to share them with you.” I mean that could be, for Frank Kern’s thing, that 
could work as a squeeze page.  
 
 Probably we could improve on it, but I just came up with it on the spur of the 
moment. H ere’s what I’m saying. “My parrot couldn’t talk, then it could. I learned these 
secrets and I’d like to share them with you.”  
 
 “I’d like to share them with you” – doesn’t that sound like the promise of a 
relationship? It does to me. The offer is sharing s ecrets. The person you’re identifying 
with “My parrot couldn’t talk” means I’m a parrot owner. I care about my bird. Maybe 
you care about yours and we have something in common.  
 
 So if you think of that in terms of you’re selling them the report and the st ream of 
information and the possibility of relationship, you’ll be in much better shape.  
 
Using Your Squeeze Page Copy To Build Credibility  
 
Now I want to take a moment to talk about credibility on your squeeze page. Now, in 
direct marketing in general, cr edibility is very, very important. And what are some 
common ways to establish credibility on a regular sales page? Well, there are all sorts, 
but the most popular would be testimonials, success stories and results.  
 
And you can very easily use credibility elements in your squeeze page. Your headline 
could be a testimonial from someone who got great results with the information you 
provided. “My Parrot Is Talking Like Crazy Since I Got This Free Report” – Signed, the 
Parrot Hunter!  
 
Your bullets, instead of regular benefit bullets could actually be facts or even small case 
studies of what people did with this information.  
 
One of the big things going through your prospect’s head when they come to your 
squeeze page is “is this for real? Can I trust these peopl e?” 
 
Anything you can do to establish real credibility on your squeeze page will pump up your 
response rate. Personally, I highly recommend that you put testimonials right on your 
squeeze page.  
 
A lot of people are going to let themselves off the hook with  a squeeze page. They’re 
going to say, “Well, I’m not asking for any money so it’s not that important.”  
 
Wrong! It’s very important. That may be your only chance to ever get that prospect to  
join your community, to become part of your list. If you don’t m ake that right first  
impression on them, it’s just as bad as losing the sale. Plus if you’re paying for the traffic,  
that’s money down the drain that could have potentially become a customer.

Squeeze Page Optimizer #2 – Your Background Color  
 
Now, when it comes to squeeze pages (like with most parts of marketing) copy really is 
king. It’s completely  possible to have an incredibly ugly squeeze page that breaks the 
rules and gets conversions because the copy is so good. But for my money you want to 
have every positive thing in your corner that you can. That means good, simple design 
that helps you get conversions.  
 
In this section I’m going to give you simple rules you need to follow to maximize the 
converting appeal of your sales pages so that your copy and your design work together to 
get people to sign up for your list, and to make you money.  
 
Backgr ound Color  
Let’s start with the background color. Now, I don’t mean the color behind the text, but 
the actual color of the page back behind the main box or table of the squeeze page. 
Generally speaking, you want to have a color that is very easy on the eye s and pretty 
neutral.  
 
In my own experience, the colors you want to use are dark blues, light or dark grays, 
white or even a black. Very neutral colors that are easy on the eyes and that make it very, 
very easy for the reader to focus right in on the messa ge of your squeeze page.  
 
This might seem like minor stuff, but you’d be surprised how much of an effect 
something like this can have on your conversion.

Selling To Women  
 
Now marketing to women, it’s interesting. I read a book by a lady named Marti Barletta,  
a very smart woman, and I actually had the opportunity to meet her. It’s called Marketing

One of the things she said is that when women are buying a perfume or clothing, they’re 
going to want more of an emotional design -y sell, but women are ac tually more thorough

In fact, women’s corpus collosum, which is the bundle of nerves that connect the right

I’m not putting men down, because I think men are great, but women are a lot smarter  
than men can give them credit for because women think differently and men can’t  
quantify that.  
 
Women don’t generally respond really well to the military dominance me taphors, like 
“You’ll crush your competition’s skull and eat his eyeballs for lunch,” which a lot of

The conclusion I came to after spending some time with Marti and then thin king about

Squeeze Page Optimizer #3: Graphics On The Squeeze Page  
 
One very simple way that you can increase the conversions of your squeeze page, 
particularly in th e consumer market is by putting a picture of an attractive woman on 
your site and to have her pointing towards the opt in box.  
 
Now, I’m not saying you need to have a woman in a bikini or anything (though for some 
male dominated markets or weight loss mark ets that can work well) but just having a 
smiling, friendly face pointing towards the opt in box can have a really nice effect on 
your conversions.  
 
In fact, adding pictures either of your self or of people who have bought from you in the  
past can do good  things for your squeeze page. Anything you can do to add color and  
interest to your page.  
 
This doesn’t mean get all busy or crazy. Quite the opposite. You want to have simple and  
elegant elements added to your page that ultimately draw attention to the  copy and to the  
opt in box. The copy is still king, after all.  
 
 
Squeeze Page Optimizer #4 – Bring Attention To The Opt -In Box  
 
Quick question, what’s the real focus of your squeeze page? What’s the part of your opt 
in page that you want your prospects to  dive for?  Well, it’s your opt -in box, and you 
want to make that opt -in box stand out as much as possible.  
 
One very simple thing you can do to draw attention to your opt in form is to put sort of a  
dashed border around it and make it look like a coupon that’s going to be cut out. This 
really makes the opt in box jump out at the reader. And really, that’s the goal. You want 
to make your opt in stand out, stand up and demand attention so that’s it’s very 
unambiguous about what you want your prospect to do.  
 
 
Squeeze Page Optimizer #5 – Your Privacy Policy  
 
This is very important. You don’t want to be shy about your privacy policy. People are 
taking a chance by giving you their email address. They’re afraid that you’re going to 
turn around and sell or give t heir information to someone and that they’re going to be 
inundated with mountains and mountains of spam.  
 
So you need to be very, very upfront about your privacy policy and in very, very plain 
English say “When you give me this information, I will guard i t with my life. I value our 
relationship and I would never do anything to put that in jeopardy.”  
 
You don’t have to say it like that, but be upfront. Make a bold statement. A statement that 
could come back to haunt you if you didn’t take care of your list.  
 
You also want to make sure that your privacy policy is very close to your sign up button. 
You want both of them in your customer’s mind at the same time.  
 
 
Squeeze Page Optimizer #6 – Your Submit Button  
 
Now we call it the submit button because that’s so rt of the standard industry language, 
but by the same token, you really don’t want to use the word “submit” on your submit 
button. That’s really, really boring. Instead you want to use your submit button as a hot 
and appealing call to action. No humans rea lly say the word “Submit” after all.  
You want to use what I call “affirmative copy.”  
 
 
 
Affirmative copy is where you’re speaking in the internal voice, the inner dialogue, of the 
prospect, where you’re speaking for them on the button.  
 
▪ Yes, I want this in formation  
▪ Yes, I want this report  
▪ Let me into the website  
▪ Grant me access to this information  
 
 It’s almost like your prospect was a cartoon character and you’re putting 
the words in the thought bubble over their head on that button and making 
them talk.
